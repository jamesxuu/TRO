Probabilistic Feedback Control
Enhances Multi‑Legged Locomotion

Juntao He, Baxi Chong, Jianfeng Lin, Zhaochen Xu, Hosain Bagheri, Esteban Flores, Daniel I. Goldman
Download PDF View on arXiv Code

Abstract

Achieving robust legged locomotion on complex terrains poses challenges due to uncertainty in robot–environment interactions. We propose a probabilistic feedback control strategy for multi-legged robots that improves locomotion speed and stability across lab and outdoor rough terrain. We use vertical body undulation as a control parameter, guided by binary foot–ground contact sensors, to mitigate environmental disturbance. Experiments on 8‑ and 12‑legged robots show 30–60 % improvement in speed with reduced variance.

Introduction

Legged robots excel in unstructured environments but often rely on heavy sensing for stability. Multi-legged systems, with inherent morphological redundancy, can traverse rugged terrains with minimal sensing. Prior work demonstrated open-loop locomotion on noisy landscapes but at slow speeds. We aim to enhance speed via a simple feedback mechanism using binary foot–ground contact sensing.

Methods

We built a modular multi‐legged robophysical platform with compliant legs and vertical/lateral body joints. Each module provides horizontal swing, vertical pitching, and leg lifting. Binary capacitive sensors at each toe detect contact. Gaits combine sinusoidal body undulation and leg stepping. A proportional controller adjusts vertical wave amplitude each cycle based on real-time contact ratio.

Results

Probabilistic models predict a linear correlation between contact ratio γ and forward speed. Lab tests on terrains with varying rugosity show vertical undulation boosts γ and speed by up to 30 %. Feedback control yields 50–60 % speed gains and 30–50 % variance reduction vs. open‐loop. Outdoor trials on mixed boulder, mud, leaves, and grass terrain achieved similar improvements.

Conclusion

We demonstrated that simple feedback of vertical body amplitude—based solely on binary foot contact—significantly enhances multi‐legged robot locomotion on rugged landscapes. Our probabilistic models guided controller design, and extensive lab and field experiments confirmed 30–60 % speed gains. This low‐sensor framework holds promise for scalable, robust legged robots in real‐world applications.
